Module 03 - Scope Breakdown System
==================================

You almost never lose money on a job because your shop rate was five dollars off. You lose money because you forgot something real. The crane. The paint. The trial fit. The rigging to flip the part. The bolts. The freight. The second delivery. The project manager burning three days chasing answers because the drawings were half-baked. That is what kills margin. And this is not just my opinion. AISC’s own fabrication benchmark says fabrication includes inspection, material handling, cutting, drilling, fit-up, welding, bolting, and shop consumables. It also treats paint and galvanizing as separate scope. So if your estimate is basically “steel plus weld hours,” you are already missing real work that the industry itself recognizes as part of the process. Welcome to Module 3. This is where estimating stops being a guess and starts becoming a system. The mistake most people make is staring at one giant set of drawings and trying to come up with one giant number.

That is how scope gets missed. That is how good shops work for free. So here is the system: Every deliverable on the job gets run through five buckets. Not the whole job in one blur. Each deliverable. Each stair. Each handrail package. Each mezzanine. Each structural frame. Run each one through the same five-bucket check. Preparation. Fabrication. Finishing. Assembly and quality control. Logistics and field readiness. That is the system. And the reason it works is simple. GAO’s cost-estimating guidance says reliable estimates need a real work breakdown structure, a defined technical baseline, clear assumptions, and all relevant costs. If you do not break the job down properly, you cannot be sure you captured everything. Let’s walk through it. Preparation is everything that has to happen before the first real fabrication hour. That means drawing review. RFIs. Shop drawings. Material buyout. Vendor follow-up. Receiving steel. Unloading trucks. Sorting stock.

Programming CNC, plasma, laser, or saw files. If a job is messy on paper, add hours here. Do not hide it in contingency fog. Put it where it belongs. If the client is slow to answer, if the drawings are incomplete, or if approvals are going to drag, that is not “free office time.” That is part of the cost of getting the job into production. GAO specifically says assumptions and ground rules should be discussed early to flush out misunderstandings and document risk. Fabrication is the floor work everybody remembers. Cutting. Drilling. Fitting. Welding. Grinding. Cleaning. But here is where a lot of estimators still miss the money. They price the weld. They forget the handling. AISC does not forget it. Material handling is explicitly part of the fabrication process. That means moving the member to the saw, to the fit-up table, to the weld bay, to the blast area, and sometimes flipping or re-positioning it just so a welder can get at the next side.

That hidden movement is what I call phantom labor. It is not phantom because it is fake. It is phantom because it disappears from bad estimates. Two forklift moves here. A crane pick there. A guy spotting a large frame while another operator eases it into position. None of that shows up on the final drawing. But your shop still pays for every minute of it. Finishing is where a lot of estimators get crushed because they assume it is minor. It is not minor. Blast. Prime. Multi-coat paint systems. Powder coat. Galvanizing. Masking. Touch-up kits. Return freight from the coater. Schedule float while the material is out of house. AISC’s fabrication benchmark explicitly excludes painting, epoxy coating, and galvanizing from the fabrication scope. That is a huge lesson. Do not bury finish inside fabrication as a vague allowance. Break it out and price it on purpose. Assembly and quality control is the bucket people forget because it does not feel like production.

But it is. Trial fit. Bolt-up in the shop. Checking hole alignment. Verifying dimensions. Inspection time. Punch-list correction. Marking pieces. Tagging hardware. Compiling paperwork. GAO warns that reliable estimates need common support elements included, not just the obvious production steps. In other words, if the job requires assembly, checkout, documentation, or management effort, it belongs in the estimate. Then you have Logistics and field readiness. This is more than shipping. It is packing. Skids. Crates. Banding. Load sequencing. Truck loading. Freight. Permits if the load is oddball. Site delivery coordination. Offload assumptions. Field hardware kits. Lift plans. Rigging allowances. And if the job is install-ready but site conditions are not, this is where you decide what you are carrying and what you are clearly excluding. That is the whole point of scope breakdown. You are not just finding cost. You are defining responsibility.

And that is what makes you competitive without being reckless. Because when a customer says, “Your number is high, take three grand out,” most shops panic and start shaving margin. That is amateur estimating. A real estimator goes back to the buckets. You say, “Fine, here are the options. If you want three thousand out, you handle pickup. Or we ship unassembled. Or we change the finish. Or we remove the trial fit. Or you provide the crane. But I am not just randomly cutting the number and hoping.” You stay competitive by removing scope, changing scope, or changing execution. Not by volunteering free work. That is the power of breaking the job down. Now here is another important point from the research. Use formulas where they help, but do not let formulas replace breakdown. GAO specifically notes that construction estimates can use cost drivers like square footage, and that higher-level parametric tools are useful when design detail is still thin.

It also says reliable estimates need current data, historical actuals, vendor quotes, and cross-checks on major cost elements. So yes, use rules of thumb. Handrail by linear foot. Mezzanine by square foot. Structural frame by tonnage or building square footage. Blast and paint by surface area. But use them as a check inside the bucket system, not as the whole estimate. That is the difference. A fast estimator says, “Mezzanines are about this much per square foot.” A sharp estimator says, “That gives me a fast benchmark, but now I still have to price prep, fab, finish, assembly, and logistics for this specific mezzanine, with this finish, this access, this schedule, and this spec.” That is how you stop underbidding weird jobs that look standard on paper. So here is the rule for this module: Bucket every deliverable before you total the job. Not one lump sum. Not one shop-hours number. Not one vague allowance. Run every package through: Preparation. Fabrication.

Finishing. Assembly and quality control. Logistics and field readiness. Then ask one last question: What exists in real life that is not obvious on the drawing? That question alone will save you a lot of money. Because the drawing shows the finished object. The estimator has to see the work required to make that object real. That is the job. That is the skill. And that is how you stop losing money on the things nobody remembered to price.
