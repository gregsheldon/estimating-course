Module 09 - Writing a Winning Quote
===================================

A quote is not just a price. A quote is a sales tool. A quote is a production plan. A quote is a risk-control document. And if the job goes wrong, the quote may become the first thing everyone goes back to when they start arguing about who was responsible for what.

So if your quote is weak, vague, or missing information, you are not just sending a number.

You are setting yourself up for confusion, scope creep, unpaid extras, and margin loss.

Welcome to Module 9.

This module is about writing a quote that actually wins work and protects you after you win it.

Because a winning quote is not the cheapest quote.

A winning quote is clear, confident, detailed, easy to understand, and built in a way that helps the customer trust you.

Here is the truth:

If your quote is vague, the customer can only compare you on price.

But if your quote is clear, now they can compare you on competence.

That is a huge difference.

A weak quote says: “Here is a number.” A strong quote says: “We understand the job. We understand the risk. We know what is included. We know what is not included. And we know how this job is going to get built.” That is how you separate yourself from the guy who just throws a number at the drawing and hopes it works. Now, let’s talk about what a winning quote actually has to do. A quote has four jobs. Number one: sell the work. Number two: define the scope. Number three: protect the margin. Number four: create the handoff if the job is awarded. If your quote only does the first one, you are in trouble. A lot of estimators treat the quote like a sales document only. They think the goal is to make the number look attractive. But the quote has to survive after the customer says yes.

It has to survive the purchase order. It has to survive the contract. It has to survive the shop floor. It has to survive the project manager. It has to survive the field crew. And if there is a dispute, it has to survive the argument. That is why the quote has to be written with discipline. Think of your quote like a fence around the job. Everything inside the fence is included. Everything outside the fence is not. If the fence is blurry, people will walk all over it. So your job is to build a clean fence. Now, the first thing your quote must do is clearly state the scope. Not buried in paragraph five. Right at the top.

What are you providing? Is it supply only? Is it supply and fabrication? Is it supply, fabrication, delivery, and install? Those are totally different jobs. A supply-only quote does not carry the same risk as a supply-and-install quote. If you do not make that clear, the customer may assume install is included. And once the customer assumes something, now you have a problem. So use clear language. For example: “This quotation includes supply and fabrication of miscellaneous steel as shown on drawings A1 through A5, revision 2.” Or: “This quotation includes supply, fabrication, delivery, and installation of handrail shown on drawings S1 and S2.” Or: “This quotation is supply only. Installation, field measuring, anchor drilling, and site work are excluded unless specifically noted.”

That kind of language matters. Because clear language prevents expensive misunderstandings. The next thing your quote needs is a clean pricing breakdown. Now, you do not always have to show every internal cost. You do not need to expose your whole estimate. But you should break the quote into logical buckets so the customer understands what they are buying. Material. Fabrication. Finishing. Installation. Equipment. Freight. Subcontractors. Engineering. Mobilization.

Taxes. Alternates. This helps the customer see that your number is not random. It also helps you negotiate without destroying your own profit. Here is the key: Never let the customer negotiate from one big lump sum. Because if they say, “Can you take $5,000 off?” and all they see is one number, the only place to take it from is your profit. But if your quote is broken into buckets, now you can negotiate scope instead of margin. You can say: “Yes, we can reduce the price by $5,000 if delivery is by others.” Or: “We can reduce the price if you remove the painted finish.” Or: “We can reduce the price if installation is done during regular hours instead of overtime.” Or:

“We can reduce the price if the site is ready and no standby time is required.” That is how professionals negotiate. They do not just cut price. They trade price for scope. That is one of the biggest secrets in this module: Do not discount. Delete. If the customer wants a lower price, remove work, remove risk, remove schedule pressure, remove finish requirements, or remove logistics. But do not remove your profit just because someone asked. Now let’s talk about assumptions. Assumptions are one of the most powerful parts of a quote. A lot of estimators think assumptions make them look weak. They do not. Assumptions make you look professional. Because assumptions tell the customer what conditions your price is based on.

For example: “Assumes clear access to work area during regular business hours.” “Assumes site is ready prior to mobilization.” “Assumes existing concrete is suitable for anchors.” “Assumes field dimensions provided by others are accurate.” “Assumes customer to provide forklift unloading unless noted otherwise.” These assumptions matter because every assumption has a cost behind it. If the site is not ready, your crew waits. If access is blocked, your install slows down. If dimensions are wrong, your shop may have to rework material. If unloading is not provided, now you need equipment and manpower. The quote has to say what world your price lives in. Because if that world changes, the price changes. That is not being difficult. That is being honest.

Now let’s talk about exclusions. Exclusions are not just fine print. Exclusions are profit protection. If something is not included, say it. Do not assume the customer knows. Do not assume “that is obvious.” Obvious is where shops lose money. Common exclusions include: Engineering. Permits. Taxes. Crane. Concrete work. Removal of existing material. Disposal.

Field painting. Touch-up by others. Surveying. Testing. Inspection. Overtime. Weekend work. Premium freight. Fire watch. Hot work permits. Site delays. Work not specifically listed. A strong exclusion sounds like this: “Excluded: engineering, permits, testing, inspection, concrete work, removal of existing material, painting, galvanizing, crane, and any work not specifically listed in the included scope above.” That last part is important:

“Any work not specifically listed.” Because the quote should not be open-ended. If the customer wants more scope, that is fine. But it needs to become a change order. Which brings us to the next major piece: Your quote needs a change-order rule. Scope creep usually does not arrive wearing a warning sign. It comes in small sentences. “Can you just add this bracket?” “Can you just drill these extra holes?” “Can you just touch up this area?” “Can you just move that rail over six inches?” “Can you just come back tomorrow instead?” Each one sounds small. But small extras add up fast.

So your quote needs to say: “Any changes to scope, quantity, schedule, site conditions, or drawings will be priced separately and must be approved in writing prior to proceeding.” That sentence protects you. Because it creates a rule before the pressure shows up. Now let’s talk about quote validity. Material prices move. Freight moves. Subcontractor pricing moves. Availability changes. So do not leave your quote open forever. For small, simple work, maybe your quote is valid for 15 or 30 days. For volatile material, custom steel, stainless, aluminum, or delayed-release jobs, your material pricing may only be valid for 7 days or subject to supplier confirmation at time of order. A clean line would be: “Quotation valid for 15 days. Material pricing subject to confirmation at time of order due to market volatility.” Or:

“Material price increases after quote validity period will be treated as an adjustment to contract value.” Again, this is not being difficult. This is protecting the business from market risk. Now here is another advanced strategy: Use alternates. A lot of estimators send one quote with one number. That is a mistake. Sometimes the best way to win is to give the customer options. Option A: quoted as drawn. Option B: value-engineered option. Option C: supply only. Option D: supply and install. Option E: galvanized finish instead of painted finish. Option F: standard material sizes instead of custom sizes. This does two things.

First, it shows the customer you are thinking. Second, it gives them a way to choose you even if the original number is too high. Instead of them saying, “You are too expensive,” they can say, “Option B works.” That is how you stay in the conversation. Here is an example: “Base price includes fabrication as drawn. Alternate 1: revise connection detail to standard bolted plate connection, deduct $4,800 and reduce lead time by one week, subject to engineering approval.” That kind of quote makes you look like a problem solver, not just another bidder. Now, here is the quote-writing secret most people miss: A good quote should make the buyer feel safe. Customers do not just buy steel. They buy reduced risk. They want to know: Will this shop understand the drawings? Will they hit the schedule? Will they bury me in change orders?

Will they communicate? Will they deliver what they promised? Will they make me look bad? Your quote should quietly answer those questions. That means your quote should be clean. Professional. Easy to read. Organized. Specific. No vague wording. No messy math. No missing revisions. No unclear scope. No “as discussed” without details. “As discussed” is dangerous.

Write down what was discussed. Because six weeks from now, nobody remembers the conversation the same way. Now let’s build the structure of a winning fabrication quote. Start with project information. Customer name. Project name. Quote number. Date. Revision number. Drawing numbers and revisions. Then scope of work. A short plain-English summary. Then inclusions. List exactly what is included. Then pricing.

Break it into useful buckets. Then alternates. Give options if there are savings, schedule improvements, or scope choices. Then assumptions. What conditions your price is based on. Then exclusions. What is not included. Then schedule and lead time. When you can start, how long fabrication takes, and what the lead time depends on. Then payment terms. Deposit, progress payments, final payment, retainage if applicable, and overdue payment terms. Then validity. How long the quote is good for. Then change-order language. How changes will be handled.

That is the basic structure. And once you have that structure, quoting becomes repeatable. Now before you send the quote, run this final check. Did I list the drawings and revision numbers? Did I clearly say supply only or supply and install? Did I list the included scope? Did I list exclusions? Did I define assumptions? Did I include freight, delivery, equipment, and subcontractors? Did I protect material escalation? Did I include payment terms? Did I include change-order language? Did I include lead time? Did I give alternates where they help the customer? Did I proofread the math?

And most importantly: If this quote gets accepted tomorrow, would I be comfortable handing it to the shop, the project manager, and the customer? If the answer is no, tighten the quote before you send it. Because after the quote is accepted, your words become commitments. That is why writing a winning quote is not about sounding fancy. It is about being clear. Clear scope. Clear price. Clear assumptions. Clear exclusions. Clear terms. Clear options. A weak quote tries to win by being cheap. A strong quote wins by creating trust. And trust is valuable.

Because when a customer trusts that you understand the work, they are less likely to treat you like a commodity. They stop comparing you only by price. They start comparing you by confidence. And that is where a good estimator has the advantage. So here is the main lesson: Your estimate builds the number. Your quote sells the number. And your wording protects the number after the job is won. Do not send naked numbers. Send a professional proposal. Define the fence around the job. Give the customer options. Protect your risk. Make the buying decision easy. That is how you write a quote that wins work and still pays.
