Module 04 - Labour Estimating
=============================

Welcome to Module 4. This module is about labour estimating. And I want to say this right at the start: Labour is where fabrication jobs are won or lost.

Material matters.

Drawings matter.

Equipment matters.

But labour is usually where the money disappears.

Because material is fairly easy to count.

You can count pounds of steel.

You can count plates.

You can count pipe.

You can count anchors.

You can call a supplier and get a price.

But labour is different.

Labour moves.

Labour changes.

Labour depends on people, access, sequence, drawings, equipment, handling, fatigue, and site conditions.

That is why I say:

Material is math.

Labour is weather.

If you do not have a system for labour, you end up estimating what you hope happens instead of what is actually going to happen.

That is the goal of this module.

I do not just want you to know how to put hours into a spreadsheet.

I want you to understand how the work actually happens.

I want you to look at a drawing and see the job before it happens.

You should be able to picture the material coming into the shop.

The saw cutting it.

The fitter laying it out.

The forklift moving it.

The welder trying to reach the joint.

The painter trying not to damage the finish.

The installer standing on site waiting for access.

That is labour estimating.

It is not just guessing hours. It is predicting human work. And if you can learn to predict human work, your estimates will get a lot sharper. The first big mistake people make is this: They think they are estimating weld time. They are not. They are estimating a full process. That process starts long before the arc turns on, and it keeps going long after the weld is finished. A weld might physically take 20 minutes to run. But that does not mean it is a 20-minute labour item. Before that weld happens, someone may have to read the drawing, find the material, pull it, move it, cut it, drill it, clean it, deburr it, lay it out, fit it, square it, clamp it, check it, flip it, tack it, and check it again. Then the weld happens. After the weld, someone may have to let it cool, clean it, grind it, inspect it, straighten it, move it, prime it, paint it, package it, load it, ship it, install it, and touch it up. So if your estimate only captures the moment the wire is feeding, you are not estimating the job. You are estimating one slice of the job. That is why shops lose money. They price the visible work. And they donate the hidden work. So here is the first rule: Do not confuse arc time with shop time. Arc time is only the middle of the story. Shop time is the whole story. To fix that, I want you to break labour into five buckets. Do not estimate labour as one giant number. Do not just say: “This feels like 80 hours.” That is not estimating. That is guessing with confidence. Instead, break labour into: Touch time. Setup time. Movement time. Control time. Friction time. Touch time is the obvious hands-on work.

Cutting. Drilling. Fitting. Welding. Grinding. Cleaning. Bolting. Anchoring. Installing. This is the work most people remember to price. But it is only one part of the job. Setup time is the work that has to happen before production really starts. Reading the print. Figuring out the sequence. Making a cut list. Setting up the saw. Setting stops. Building a jig. Laying out the first piece. Squaring the first assembly. Proving the process. Setup time is dangerous because people treat it like planning. But it is still labour. If the shop spends three hours figuring out how to build something, that is labour. If the foreman spends time explaining the job to the crew, that is labour. If the fitter builds a temporary jig, that is labour. The third bucket is movement time. This is where a lot of profit disappears quietly. Movement time is walking to the rack. Pulling material. Moving material with a forklift. Staging parts. Flipping an assembly. Re-clamping. Moving it from saw to drill.

Moving it from drill to fit-up. Moving it from fit-up to weld. Moving it from weld to paint. Moving it from paint to shipping. Moving it from the truck to the install location. Movement does not feel like production. But it burns hours. And here is a rule I want you to remember: If the part is too big to stay on one table, or too heavy to move by hand, stop pricing only fabrication and start pricing handling. Large work does not just take longer to weld. It takes longer to move, flip, stage, protect, load, and install. The fourth bucket is control time. Control time is checking and verification. Measuring. Squaring. Leveling. Trial fitting. Inspection. Paperwork. Photos. Sign-offs. Quality checks. Control time matters because bad control creates rework. And rework is one of the most expensive types of labour. Because you already paid once to do it wrong. Now you are paying again to make it right. The fifth bucket is friction time. Friction time is real life. Bad access. Awkward posture. Overhead work. Ladder work. Waiting for material. Waiting for answers. Waiting for a crane.

Waiting for another trade. Heat. Cold. Fatigue. Interruptions. Congested work areas. Poor sequencing. Small rework. Drawing confusion. Missing dimensions. Friction is the labour nobody wants to admit exists. But it exists. And if you do not price it, you pay for it. So here is the basic labour formula: Total labour hours equals touch time, plus setup time, plus movement time, plus control time, plus friction time. That is the foundation. Not: “How long will this take to weld?” But: “How long will it take real people to move this job from raw material to finished work?” That is the shift. Weak estimators price tasks. Strong estimators price the path. Now let’s talk about how a shop actually works. A job does not magically go from drawing to finished product. It moves through steps. First, there is the estimate. Then the job is awarded. Then drawings are reviewed. Missing information gets clarified. Material gets ordered. Material gets received. Material gets staged. Then it may be cut, drilled, punched, coped, notched, deburred, cleaned, fitted, tacked, checked, welded, cleaned again, ground, inspected, straightened, painted, galvanized, packaged, loaded, shipped, installed, touched up, and final checked. Every time the job moves from one step to another, labour is hiding there.

That does not mean every job needs a huge number of hours. It means every job needs to be thought through. A small bracket may flow fast. A big stair may move slow. A handrail may look easy until you realize there are multiple sections, returns, base plates, anchors, paint, delivery, and install. A roof ladder may look simple until you realize access is elevated, the wall condition is unknown, layout has to be right, and the crew may need lift time. This is why good estimators mentally walk the job. They do not just read the drawing. They imagine the work happening. I call this the mental movie method. Before you put hours on a job, play the job like a movie in your head. Ask yourself: Where does the material start? Who touches it first? How does it get to the saw? How many cuts are there? Does it need drilling? Does it need coping? Does it need layout? Can one person handle it? Does it need two people? Does it need a forklift? Does it fit on the table? Does it need to be flipped? Can it be welded flat? Does it need overhead welding? Does it need a jig? Does it need inspection? Does it need paint? Does it need to dry? Does it need to be protected for shipping? Can it be installed as one piece? Does it need to be broken into sections? Is there room to unload? Is there room to work?

What could stop the crew? That is how you estimate. You play the movie. And if you cannot see the movie, that is your signal. Do not pretend you know. Ask somebody who does. Ask the foreman. Ask the fitter. Ask the welder. Ask the painter. Ask the installer. Ask the project manager. The best estimators are not the ones who act like they know everything. The best estimators are the ones who know what questions to ask. Now, here is another important rule: Count events, not feelings. Bad estimating sounds like this: “That should not take long.” “That looks easy.” “We have done something like that before.” “Give it 40 hours.” That is not estimating. That is hoping. Good estimating counts events. An event is any action that costs time. One cut is an event. One hole is an event. One cope is an event. One fit-up is an event. One weld start is an event. One flip is an event. One forklift move is an event. One layout is an event. One inspection is an event. One anchor location is an event.

One field adjustment is an event. One touch-up is an event. One return trip is an event. When you count events, labour becomes less mysterious. You are not pricing “a handrail.” You are pricing the actions required to make and install the handrail. How many posts? How many rails? How many pickets? How many cuts? How many welds? How many base plates? How many holes? How many anchors? How many sections? How many field joints? How many trips? How many coats of paint? How many install locations? That is real estimating. Let me give you a simple example. Say you have 50 brackets. A weak estimator says: “They are small brackets. That should not take long.” A strong estimator breaks it down. There is one-time work: Review the drawing. Confirm the material. Set up the saw. Set up the drill pattern. Make the first piece. Check the first piece. Adjust if needed. Then there is repeat work: Fifty cuts.

One hundred holes. Fifty deburrs. Fifty fit-ups. Fifty welds. Fifty clean-ups. Then there is batch work: Final inspection. Stacking. Packaging. Loadout. Now you are estimating the job like a process. Not like a feeling. Maybe each weld only takes a few minutes. But the job is not just welding. The job is processing 50 pieces through the shop. That is the difference. Now let’s talk about one of the biggest secrets in labour estimating: Separate one-time work from repeat work. Do not bury setup into every piece. But do not forget setup completely. Both mistakes are expensive. One-time work includes drawing review, planning, tooling, jig setup, saw setup, drill setup, first-piece layout, first-piece prove-out, foreman review, and clarifications. Repeat work includes each cut, each hole, each weld, each part, each assembly, and each repeated install point. Here is the formula: Batch hours equal setup hours, plus first-piece prove-out, plus repeat hours times quantity, plus quality control, plus loadout. This matters because one custom stair is not estimated the same way as ten matching stairs. The first stair carries more thinking, more layout, and more proving. The next nine should usually get faster if they are truly the same. But be careful. They only get faster if they are actual repeats. If every stair has a different height, a different landing, a different field condition, or a different connection, then they are not true repeats. Do not give a repeat discount to non-repeat work. That is how you lose money. Now let’s talk about where shops lose money on labour. Most labour losses come from a few places.

They price arc time instead of full shop time. They ignore handling. They forget setup. They underprice layout. They treat field work like shop work. They forget quality control. They use pay rate instead of loaded labour rate. They ignore fatigue. They forget that equipment still needs people. And they guess instead of asking the shop. Those are the traps. But a good estimator does more than price the problem. A good estimator helps the shop see the problem. If everything gets tracked under one labour code called “fabrication,” you learn almost nothing. A better shop separates labour codes. Cutting. Drilling. Fit-up. Welding. Grinding. Painting. Install. Handling. Rework. That way, after the job, you can see where the time actually went. Another fix is separating setup from production. Track setup separately. That helps you understand what the repeat work really costs. It also tells you whether the shop is losing time doing the work, or losing time getting ready to do the work. Another fix is using job travellers. A job traveller follows the work through the shop. It tells the crew what the job is, what material is being used, what drawing revision is current, what the finish is, what the weld requirements are, what the inspection points are, and when it is due. A good traveller reduces confusion. And confusion is expensive. Another fix is saving jig and fixture notes.

If the shop builds something once, do not let that knowledge disappear. Take photos. Save measurements. Save setup notes. Write down what worked. Write down what did not. The second time should be faster. But only if the knowledge survives. Another fix is holding a pre-fab meeting. For risky work, get the estimator, foreman, fitter, welder, painter, and installer aligned before the work starts. Ask: What is the hardest part? What needs clarification? What should be built first? What needs a jig? What needs field verification? What can go wrong? That meeting can save days. Another fix is post-job review. After the job is done, compare estimated hours to actual hours. But do not stop there. Ask why. Was the estimate wrong? Was the drawing bad? Was the material late? Was the shop inefficient? Was there rework? Was the job sequenced badly? Was the site worse than expected? That feedback is how a shop gets smarter. Without post-job review, every estimate is just another fresh guess. Now let’s talk about site work. Because site labour can destroy a good shop estimate. The site is uncontrolled compared to the shop. In the shop, you usually control your tools, benches, forklifts, power, lighting, material location, and workflow.

In the field, you may control almost none of that. Site work includes loading at the shop, travel, unloading, orientation, safety paperwork, moving material to the work area, setting up tools, getting power, getting permits, coordinating with other trades, layout, drilling, anchoring, welding or bolting, fire watch if required, touch-up, inspection, cleanup, demobilization, and travel back. That is why field install should never be priced as: “Two guys for a few hours.” That phrase has killed a lot of margin. Use this field labour formula: Total field hours equal travel, plus mobilization, plus access, plus layout, plus install, plus control, plus friction, plus demobilization. Travel means how long it takes to get there, how many workers are going, how many trips are needed, where they can park, and whether they can unload close to the work. Mobilization means loading tools, loading material, loading lifts, loading anchors, loading consumables, driving, unloading, carrying tools to the work area, setting up cords, setting up tools, getting permits, and attending orientation. Access means where the work actually is. Ground level is different from ladder work. Ladder work is different from lift work. Lift work is different from scaffold. Scaffold is different from roof work. And all of that is different from working in a tight room, over equipment, in a public space, or around other trades. Access changes everything. Layout means figuring out where the work goes. Are the dimensions clear? Are the walls straight? Is the concrete good? Are embeds in the right place? Are anchor locations known? Does anything need field measuring? Install is the actual install work: Bolting. Welding. Drilling. Setting. Leveling. Plumbing. Aligning. Tightening. Touch-up. Control is inspection, photos, sign-off, torque checks, weld checks, cleanup, and deficiency walkthroughs. Friction is weather, waiting, other trades, bad drawings, missing access, wrong anchors, poor concrete, blocked work areas, safety restrictions, customer interruptions, noise limits, dust control, and fire watch.

Demobilization is packing tools, removing equipment, cleaning the area, loading the truck, returning to the shop, unloading, and putting tools away. That is the full site picture. Now let’s talk about labour difficulty. Not all labour hours are equal. A weld on a bench at chest height is not the same as a weld on a ladder. A part that stays on one table is not the same as a part that needs two people and a forklift every time it moves. A clean repetitive shop job is not the same as a one-off field-fit job with tight tolerance and constant measuring. That is why we use labour multipliers. A multiplier is not a magic number. It is a thinking tool. For open bench work, repeated parts, and easy access, your multiplier may be 1.00. For frequent re-clamping, awkward fit-up, tighter checking, or mild difficulty, maybe you are in the 1.10 to 1.25 range. For large assemblies, repeated flips, ladder work, congested areas, or extra alignment, maybe you are in the 1.25 to 1.40 range. For overhead work, poor access, field-fit conditions, high inspection load, tight site coordination, or difficult conditions, maybe you are in the 1.35 to 1.60 range. For unknown fit, night shift, rush work, premium work, long shifts, weather exposure, or high rework risk, you may be 1.50 or higher. But do not just add a multiplier because you are nervous. Name the risk. Price the risk. Document the risk. If the multiplier is higher, you should be able to explain why. Bad access. Overhead welding. Heavy handling. Multiple moves. Unknown site dimensions. High inspection. Tight schedule. That is disciplined estimating. Now let’s talk about fatigue. Almost nobody prices fatigue properly. There is a bad habit where people assume ten hours of work is just eight hours plus a little more. But people do not work like machines. Long shifts create fatigue. Fatigue lowers output. Fatigue increases mistakes.

Fatigue increases rework risk. So if you are pricing overtime, weekend shutdowns, night shift, rush fabrication, or long install days, do not just add the wage premium. Add the productivity haircut. The labour rate goes up. But the output per hour may go down. That double hit is why rushed jobs can destroy margin. Now let’s talk about loaded labour. Paycheck wage is not labour cost. If a worker gets paid $30 an hour, that does not mean the shop’s labour cost is $30 an hour. The real cost may include benefits, payroll burden, insurance, workers compensation, supervision, shop overhead, small tools, consumables, training, downtime, utilities, and support staff. Then the sell rate has to be higher again because the business still needs profit. So there are three numbers you need to understand. Wage rate. Loaded cost rate. Sell rate. The wage rate is what the worker gets paid. The loaded cost rate is what the worker actually costs the business. The sell rate is what you charge the customer. Do not mix those up. A shop can have the right hours and still lose money if the labour rate is wrong. Now let’s talk about quality and rework. Fabrication is not perfectly repeatable like printing paper. People matter. Fit-up matters. Material condition matters. Access matters. Operator experience matters. Drawing clarity matters. Environment matters. So certain jobs need extra time for quality, inspection, and possible correction. Ask yourself: Have we built this before? Has this crew built this before? Is the material familiar? Are the tolerances tight?

Is the weld spec higher than normal? Is there inspection? Is there testing? Are dimensions field verified? Is the finish exposed? Is the customer picky? Is there a high cost if this does not fit? If the answer raises risk, carry time for checking, trial fit, inspection, documentation, and minor correction. The cheapest time to pay for rework is in the estimate. The most expensive time is after delivery, when the job is late and everyone is mad. Now I want to give you the questions to ask when you do not know enough. You do not have to know everything. But you do need to know who to ask. If you are unsure how the shop will build it, ask the foreman: How would you build this? What is the first step? What part will slow the guys down? Will this need a jig? Can one person handle it? How many times will it need to be flipped? Can it stay on one table? Will it need forklift handling? Can it be welded flat? Will it pull or warp? Will it need straightening? What tolerance should I worry about? Have we built something like this before? What went wrong last time? If you are unsure about fit-up or welding, ask the fitter or welder: Is the fit-up simple or awkward? Can this be clamped easily? Are the welds easy to reach? Are any welds overhead or out of position? Will the weld sequence matter? Will heat distortion be an issue?

Is grinding required for appearance? Is this production work or fussy custom work? How much cleanup should be expected? If you are unsure about finishing, ask the painter or finisher: Does this need blast, prime, paint, powder coat, or galvanizing? Can it be painted as one piece? Does it need to be broken down? Are there surfaces that are hard to reach? Is drying or cure time an issue? Will handling after paint be a problem? Will touch-up be required after install? If you are unsure about site work, ask the installer: How do we get material to the work area? Can a truck get close? Is there a laydown area? Do we need a lift? Do we need scaffold? Are we working on ladders? Is the work overhead? Are we drilling into concrete, steel, wood, or masonry? Are anchors specified? Is the existing structure straight? Do we need field measurements? Are other trades in the way? Is the site occupied? Are there shutdown hours? Do we need fire watch? Do we need permits? How many trips will this take? If you are unsure about customer or general contractor responsibilities, ask: Are drawings final? Are dimensions field verified? Who is responsible for embeds? Who supplies anchors? Who supplies access equipment?

Are there site-specific safety requirements? Are there restricted work hours? Is parking available? Is unloading available? Who provides power? Who provides layout control lines? Who signs off when complete? What is excluded? What is assumed? Those questions protect the estimate. They also protect the shop. Now let’s walk through a simple handrail example. A weak estimate says: “Handrail is easy. We will allow some shop hours and a day install.” That might be right. But it might be very wrong. A strong estimate breaks down the work. In the shop, someone has to review the drawings, confirm rail height, confirm post spacing, confirm code requirements, order pipe, receive pipe, cut rails, cut posts, cut returns, cut base plates, drill base plates, fit the posts, tack the assembly, check height, check spacing, weld it, clean it, grind it if required, prime or paint it, let the finish dry, protect the finish, and load it for delivery. Then in the field, someone has to load the truck, travel to site, unload, move material to the install location, confirm layout, mark anchor locations, drill anchors, set posts, level rails, tighten anchors, touch up paint, clean the area, do a final walkthrough, and return to the shop. Now ask the real questions. Is it straight rail or sloped rail? Are there stairs? Are there returns? Are posts core-drilled or base-plated? Is the concrete existing? Is the concrete good? Are anchors included? Is field measuring required? Is there a lift? Is access easy? Is it inside or outside? Is the finish exposed? Does install happen during normal working hours? Are there other trades in the way? That is how you see the job before it happens.

So here is the full method I want you to use on every job. First, break the scope into work areas. Shop fabrication. Finish. Delivery. Field install. Equipment. Engineering. Subcontractors. Second, break each work area into events. Cuts. Holes. Welds. Fit-ups. Flips. Moves. Inspections. Trips. Anchors. Lifts. Third, separate setup from repeat work. One-time setup. First-piece prove-out. Repeat work. Batch closeout. Fourth, add movement and handling. Material movement. Forklift movement. Crane support. Rehandling. Loadout. Site movement. Fifth, add control. Measuring. Inspection.

Documentation. Sign-off. Trial fit. Sixth, add friction. Access. Awkward work. Weather. Overtime. Unknowns. Other trades. Tight tolerances. Rush schedule. Seventh, apply the right labour rate. Use loaded cost for cost. Use sell rate for price. Do not confuse wage with labour cost. Eighth, document your assumptions. For example: Assumes clear site access. Assumes normal working hours. Assumes anchors by others. Assumes field dimensions provided before fabrication. Assumes no hidden steel conflicts. Assumes one mobilization. Assumes lift supplied by others. Assumes no premium time. Assumptions matter because the estimate is only as good as the assumptions behind it. So here is the closing lesson. Labour is a story. Every job has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Bad estimators only price the obvious work. Good estimators price the whole story. They price the setup. They price the movement. They price the checking.

They price the interruptions. They price the finish. They price the return trip. They price the awkward access. They price the human reality. That is why good labour estimating almost feels like watching the future. You look at a drawing and you can see the shop floor. You can see the fitter searching for a dimension. You can see the forklift moving the assembly. You can see the welder trying to reach an awkward joint. You can see the installer waiting for access. You can see the painter needing extra handling because the finish cannot be scratched. You can see the job before it costs you money. That is the skill. Material is usually easier to count. Labour is where fabrication jobs bleed. But labour does not have to be a mystery. If you break it into touch time, setup time, movement time, control time, and friction time, you can start to control it. If you separate one-time work from repeat work, your batch pricing gets sharper. If you count events instead of feelings, your estimates become more realistic. If you ask the right questions, you do not need to know everything yourself. You just need to know how to pull the right information from the people who do. That is what professional estimators do. They do not guess lower. They understand better. And the shop that understands the work better is the shop that protects its margin. So from now on, do not ask: “How long will it take to weld?” Ask: “How will this job actually happen?” Then price that reality. That is labour estimating.
