Module 01 - The Real Role of an Estimator
=========================================

Before we get into the training, I want you to know where this comes from. I’m not teaching this from theory. I’ve spent over 50,000 hours in the trades, working through just about every side of fabrication work. I’ve been on the tools. I’ve fit steel. I’ve welded. I’ve built jobs. I’ve dealt with drawings that were missing information. I’ve seen what happens when a quote looks fine in the office and then falls apart once the job hits the floor. Over time, I moved from building the work to pricing the work, managing the work, and helping companies win the right work. Last year alone, I helped one company win more than $16 million in fabrication work. And what I learned is this: The shops that stay profitable are not always the shops with the lowest number. They’re the shops that understand the work before the work starts. That is not just my opinion.

The estimating guidance used by serious cost organizations says reliable estimates have to be comprehensive, well-documented, accurate, and credible. That means defining the scope, documenting assumptions, breaking the work down properly, and testing where the risk is. And in steel, that matters even more than people think. AISC is very clear that fabrication is not just steel and welding. It includes inspection, handling, cutting, drilling, fit-up, welding, bolting, and sometimes surface prep. It also makes clear that coating and galvanizing are separate scope items, which means if you assume the finish wrong, you can be wrong in a big way before the job even starts. That is what this course is about. I’m going to show you how to estimate fabrication work the way it actually gets built. Not textbook theory. Real-world estimating. The kind that helps you price with confidence, protect your shop, and win work without buying bad jobs.

Most shop owners are looking in the wrong place for lost profit. They walk the floor, they see a guy taking too long on a part, and they think that’s where the job went sideways. Sometimes it is. But a lot of the time, the bigger mistake happened earlier. It happened at the estimator’s desk. It happened when the job was priced off the drawing title instead of the real scope. It happened when somebody counted the obvious steel, missed the hidden labour, guessed the install, and sent the quote anyway. That is the first big idea in this course: The quote is not just a price. The quote is the first version of the job plan. A real estimate has to include more than steel and weld time. The best estimating guidance says a reliable estimate has to include all costs, be based on a real baseline, document assumptions, and account for risk.

And in fabrication, AISC reminds us that the work includes handling, cutting, drilling, fit-up, welding, bolting, and more, not just the finished steel you see on the drawing. So let me simplify this. There are two ways to quote work. The first way is what I call counting steel and hoping. You look at the prints, count the major members, throw on a shop rate, add markup, and hit send. The second way is how good estimators work. You build the job in your head before the shop ever builds it in steel. You ask: What exactly does the spec require? What work is in the shop? What work is in the field? What is included? What is missing? What is probably going to show up after award? What do I need to qualify right now so I’m not arguing about it later? That is the real job of an estimator. You are not just a pricing clerk. You are the financial gatekeeper of the company. You are the last person who can kill a bad assumption before it turns into rework, delay, or lost margin.

And one of the biggest mistakes I see is this: people price to what they think the job is, instead of what the drawings, notes, schedules, and specifications actually say. If the spec says galvanized, do not price primer. If it says stainless, do not price carbon steel. If it says delegated design, testing, special inspection, submittals, mockups, field verification, or after-hours work, those are not just words on paper. Those are cost drivers. RSMeans specifically warns estimators to read the specs closely because submittals, quality requirements, temporary facilities, closeout requirements, and mobilization costs are easy to miss and can materially affect the estimate. Here is a practical way to think about every job. Break it into three buckets. The first bucket is base scope. That is the work clearly required by the drawings, notes, details, schedules, and spec. The second bucket is probable extras.

These are the things that are not always drawn well, but experienced people know often show up: extra trips, field measurement, touch-up, bad access, rebar in anchor holes, more setup time, longer lift time, longer crane time, coordination delays, or changed conditions. The third bucket is controlled exclusions. These are the things you are not carrying unless specifically called for: engineering, permits, special inspection, patching by others, concrete by others, power by others, traffic control, shutdown work, premium time, and so on. That one habit alone will make your estimates better. Why? Because it forces you to decide whether each risk gets priced, qualified, or excluded. Now let’s talk about one of the biggest secrets in estimating: Do not price the object. Price the actions required to create the object. A handrail is not “just a handrail.” It is material type, finish, posts, returns, holes, welds, grinding, baseplates, anchors, install, and touch-up.

A mezzanine is not “just square footage.” It is columns, beams, joists, deck or grating, stairs, rail, kick plate, connections, baseplates, anchors, delivery, erection, and usually more coordination than people think. A structural package is not “just tons.” It is member prep, holes, clips, connection material, detailing, shop handling, loading, trucking, erection equipment, and field conditions. That is why I use formulas and breakdowns. For early screening, I’ll use quick parametric checks: handrails by linear foot, mezzanines by square foot, and structural packages by tonnage or pounds per square foot. But that is just the first pass. GAO’s estimating guide is very clear here: parametric methods are useful for budgetary estimates and cross-checks, while engineering build-up is the detailed method that estimates labor and materials at the lowest level when there is enough detail to do it right.

That is exactly how good fabrication estimating should work: use the quick rule of thumb to get close, then use a bottom-up build to get real. And when you go bottom-up, this is where money gets saved. Because the hidden costs are rarely in the giant beam everyone noticed. They are in the things people assume are too small to matter. RSMeans points out that connection material alone can justify roughly a 10% weight allowance for nuts, bolts, washers, connection angles, and plates. It also notes that equipment mobilization is separate from rental cost, that painting is heavily driven by surface preparation, and that estimators need to add drilling into concrete and certain fasteners separately when they are not included. That is exactly the kind of stuff that makes one shop think a job was “fine” while another shop knows it was underpriced from day one. So when I say jobs can start losing money at the estimator’s desk, I do not mean estimators are the problem.

I mean estimating is where profit is either protected or exposed. If the estimate is clear, the shop has a plan. If the estimate is vague, the shop inherits confusion. If the assumptions are documented, the company has a basis for changes. If the assumptions stay in somebody’s head, the company ends up eating work it never meant to include. That is why this module matters. I want you to stop thinking of estimating as clerical work. It is not clerical work. It is strategy. It is risk control. It is production planning before production starts. And it is one of the highest-leverage skills in the entire business. By the end of this course, I want you to be able to look at a job and not just say, “What does this steel cost?” I want you to be able to say, “What will this job actually take, what is likely to go wrong, and how do I price it so the company still makes money when real life shows up?” That is real estimating. And that is where profitable fabrication work starts.
