Module 08 - Building the Final Price
====================================

In this module, we are going to talk about one of the most important parts of estimating: Building the final price.

This is where a lot of fabricators lose money without realizing it.

They may have the material right.

They may have the labour close.

They may even understand the scope.

But then they build the final price wrong.

They use the wrong markup.

They confuse markup with margin.

They use a labour rate that is too low.

They forget overhead.

They ignore risk.

Or they cut the price at the end just to win the job, without understanding what they just gave away.

And that is dangerous.

Because a job you win at a loss is not a win.

It is unpaid stress with a purchase order attached to it.

The goal of final pricing is not just to be cheap enough to win.

The goal is to build a number the company can actually fabricate, install, invoice, and profit from.

That means the final price has to do three things:

Cover the true cost.

Protect against risk.

And produce the margin the company needs.

If one of those three is missing, the job is in trouble before it even starts.

So let’s start with one of the biggest profit killers in estimating:

Markup versus margin.

A lot of people use those words like they mean the same thing.

They do not.

Markup is what you add on top of cost. Margin is what you keep out of the final selling price. That difference matters a lot. Let’s use a simple example. Say a job costs you $10,000. That includes material, labour, equipment, freight, and everything else you need to deliver the work. Now let’s say you add 20% markup. Twenty percent of $10,000 is $2,000. So your selling price becomes $12,000. A lot of people would say: “Good. We made 20%.” No, you did not. You added 20% markup. But your margin is not 20%. Your profit is $2,000. Your selling price is $12,000. So your margin is $2,000 divided by $12,000. That equals 16.7%. So a 20% markup only gives you a 16.7% gross margin. That is one of those quiet estimating mistakes that damages profit over and over again. The estimator thinks they priced the job at 20%. The owner thinks the job has 20% in it. But the job only has 16.7%. Then the work starts. Material runs a little higher. The field crew loses half a day. The shop burns extra hours.

A delivery is missed. Now that 16.7% disappears fast. So here are the formulas you need to know. Markup percentage equals: Selling price minus cost, divided by cost. Margin percentage equals: Selling price minus cost, divided by selling price. Markup compares profit to cost. Margin compares profit to selling price. When you are running a business, margin is usually the number you care about, because margin tells you how much of the revenue you actually kept. Now let’s say you want a true 20% margin on a $10,000 cost. You do not add 20%. To get a true 20% margin, you divide the cost by 1 minus the margin. The formula is: Selling price equals cost divided by 1 minus target margin. So if the cost is $10,000 and you want a 20% margin, you divide by 0.80. $10,000 divided by 0.80 equals $12,500. That means a true 20% margin requires a $12,500 selling price. That is a 25% markup. That is why this matters. On a small job, it might look like a small difference. On a large job, it becomes serious. If you add 20% markup to a $500,000 cost, the selling price is $600,000. Your profit is $100,000. Your margin is 16.7%. But if you wanted a true 20% margin, the selling price should be $625,000. That is a $25,000 difference.

That could be the difference between a good job and a job that looks good until closeout. So here is the rule: Do not guess the markup. Start with the margin you need, then calculate the selling price properly. For a 10% margin, divide by 0.90. For a 15% margin, divide by 0.85. For a 20% margin, divide by 0.80. For a 25% margin, divide by 0.75. For a 30% margin, divide by 0.70. For a 35% margin, divide by 0.65. For a 40% margin, divide by 0.60. That is how you build the price. But before you apply margin, you need to know your true cost. And this is the second major problem. A lot of shops do not actually know their real labour cost. They know what they pay the welder. But the wage is not the true cost of the labour hour. If a welder makes $35 an hour, that does not mean the labour hour costs the company $35. That is just the wage. The real cost includes payroll taxes, workers’ compensation, vacation pay, holidays, benefits, insurance, training, PPE, uniforms, shop supplies, small tools, supervision, admin support, and payroll processing. Then you have overhead. Rent. Utilities. Office staff. Estimating time. Project management. Software.

Equipment leases. Maintenance. Insurance. Shop trucks. Forklifts. Cranes. Welding machines. Compressors. Phones. Internet. Accounting. Safety. All of that has to be paid for. And it has to be recovered through the work. That means every billable hour needs to carry its share of overhead. So when you build a shop rate, you are not asking: “What do we pay the welder?” You are asking: “What does one productive billable hour actually cost this company?” That is the number you need. Let’s use a simple example. Say the welder’s base wage is $35 per hour. After payroll burden, benefits, vacation, insurance, and employee costs, maybe that labour really costs $50 per hour. Now add overhead. Let’s say your shop overhead works out to $30 per billable hour. Now that $35 wage has turned into an $80 true cost before profit. If you are charging the customer $75 per hour because that “sounds about right,” you are not making money.

You are losing money. The owner is subsidizing the customer’s job. And the dangerous part is that the shop can be busy while losing money. Busy does not mean profitable. A full shop does not mean a healthy shop. You can have welders working, trucks moving, material coming in, invoices going out, and still be bleeding cash because the labour rate was built wrong. That is why every estimator needs to understand burdened labour. A fully burdened labour rate includes the real cost of employing the person and operating the business around that person. The basic formula is: Gross wages plus payroll burdens plus benefits plus overhead allocated, divided by billable hours. Another simple overhead formula is: Total fixed overhead divided by total billable hours equals overhead per hour. For example: $300,000 annual overhead divided by 10,000 billable hours equals $30 per hour. That $30 has to be added to the labour cost. Not later. Not maybe. Not only if someone remembers. It needs to be built into the rate. Now here is another trap: Using 2,080 hours per year as if every employee is 100% productive and billable. A full-time employee may be paid for around 2,080 hours per year. But that does not mean you get 2,080 billable hours. People take vacation. They have holidays. They attend safety meetings. They clean the shop. They move material.

They wait for drawings. They wait for answers. They fix tools. They unload trucks. They rework mistakes. They train people. They have downtime between jobs. A lot of that time is necessary, but it is not always billable to a specific job. So if you calculate your labour rate using 2,080 hours, you may be lying to yourself. The more honest question is: How many productive billable hours do we really get? That is utilization. If you pay someone for 2,080 hours but only get 1,350 billable hours, the true cost per billable hour jumps. For example, say the annual cost tied to one employee and their share of overhead is $150,000. If you divide that by 2,080 hours, the cost looks like about $72 per hour. But if the real billable hours are only 1,350, the cost is about $111 per hour. That is a massive difference. If your spreadsheet is quoting labour at $75 per hour because it assumes 2,080 productive hours, you think you are safe. But you are actually selling a $111 cost for $75. That is how shops go broke while everyone is working. So when you build the final price, the first question is not: “What hourly rate sounds normal?” The question is: “What hourly rate actually covers our cost?” Then after that, you ask: “What does the market allow?” You need both.

Your cost tells you your lowest safe number. The market tells you what the job may sell for. Let’s say your fully burdened labour cost is $65 per hour, and local fabricators are billing around $100 per hour for similar work. That is a strong position. You may be able to price at $90 or $95 and still make money. You may be able to win strategically. You may have room to absorb some risk. But if your true break-even is $95 per hour and the market is only paying $90 to $100, now you have a problem. And the answer is not simply: “We need to be cheaper.” Cheaper does not fix a broken cost structure. If your break-even is too close to the market rate, the real fix is improving the business. Better planning. Less rework. Better material handling. Less downtime. Better equipment. Better scheduling. Better supervision. Cleaner drawings. Better estimating. Lower overhead where possible. Because if the cost structure is broken, cutting the price just makes the damage worse. This is one of the most important lessons in estimating: Estimating is not about getting the number low enough to win. Estimating is about finding the number where the job is worth winning. There is a big difference.

Now let’s talk about the full price stack. When you build a final price, you need to make sure every cost bucket is included before margin is applied. A proper fabrication estimate may include: Material. Material waste. Freight. Shop labour. Install labour. Consumables. Hardware. Equipment. Subcontractors. Engineering or detailing. Mobilization. Trucking. Site supervision. Project management. Safety requirements. Testing or inspection. Permits or documentation. Overhead. Risk. Contingency. Profit. And taxes, depending on how the quote is structured. Every job will not have all of those items. But the estimator needs to think through all of them.

The mistake is not always that the formula is wrong. Sometimes the formula is right, but the cost is incomplete. For example, maybe you priced the steel, shop labour, and install labour. But you forgot freight. You forgot lift rental. You forgot engineering review. You forgot site orientation. You forgot safety paperwork. You forgot travel time. Then you apply the correct margin formula. The math may be right, but the price is still wrong because the cost was incomplete. That is why I like to think of final pricing in layers. Layer one is direct cost. Material, labour, hardware, equipment, and subcontractors. Layer two is support cost. Freight, handling, layout, detailing, project management, supervision, safety, and mobilization. Layer three is business cost. Overhead, shop burden, admin, insurance, facilities, and equipment ownership. Layer four is risk. Bad drawings, tight schedule, difficult access, weather, coordination issues, customer delays, night work, shutdown work, unknown site conditions, and unclear scope. Layer five is profit. The margin the company must keep after the job is done. A lot of bad quotes happen because someone prices layer one and then throws markup on it. That is not enough. Final price is not material plus labour plus markup. Final price is the complete cost of delivering the work, plus the profit required to make the job worth taking. Now let’s talk about risk and contingency. Some people get nervous about contingency because they think it means padding the job.

It does not. Contingency is not random padding. Contingency is pricing known uncertainty. There is a big difference. Padding is guessing. Protection is based on risk. If the drawings are clean, the scope is clear, access is easy, and the work is repetitive, risk may be low. But if the drawings are missing dimensions, the site conditions are unknown, access is tight, other trades are in the way, the schedule is compressed, and the deadline cannot move, the risk is higher. That risk has a cost. Someone is going to pay for it. Either the customer pays for it in the quote, or the shop pays for it later in lost margin. A good estimator does not ignore risk. A good estimator names it and decides how to handle it. You can handle risk by adding contingency. You can qualify the scope. You can exclude unknowns. You can carry an allowance. You can ask questions before bid closing. You can separate optional scope. You can price alternates. You can state assumptions clearly in the proposal. Not every risk should be buried in the number. Sometimes the best protection is clear proposal language. For example, if the drawings do not show embeds, do not quietly guess. Ask the question. If no answer comes, qualify the quote. Say embedded plates are excluded unless specifically noted.

If site access is unknown, state that the price assumes clear access during regular hours. If a lift is included, state what type of lift and how many days. If paint touch-up is excluded, say that. If engineering is not included, say that. A clear quote protects the margin. A vague quote invites arguments. And arguments after award usually cost the fabricator money. Now let’s walk through a simple pricing example. Say we have a small fabrication job. Material is $12,000. Material waste and small extras are $1,000. Freight is $800. Shop labour is 120 hours. Install labour is 40 hours. The fully burdened shop labour cost is $75 per hour. The fully burdened install labour cost is $90 per hour. So shop labour is: 120 hours times $75, which equals $9,000. Install labour is: 40 hours times $90, which equals $3,600. Now add the rest: Equipment and tools: $1,500. Consumables: $750. Project management and admin allowance: $1,200. Mobilization: $1,500. Now total the cost. Material: $12,000.

Waste and extras: $1,000. Freight: $800. Shop labour: $9,000. Install labour: $3,600. Equipment: $1,500. Consumables: $750. Project management: $1,200. Mobilization: $1,500. Total cost: $31,350. Now let’s say we want a 25% gross margin. We do not add 25%. We divide by 0.75. $31,350 divided by 0.75 equals $41,800. That is the selling price needed for a true 25% margin. Now compare that to the wrong way. If someone simply added 25% markup to $31,350, the selling price would be $39,187.50. That sounds close, but it is over $2,600 lower. And the margin would only be 20%, not 25%. That difference matters. On a bigger job, it can be the difference between making money and just staying busy. Now after you build the price, you need to check it. A good estimator does not blindly trust the first number. You need quote checks. Check the total margin. Does the final selling price actually produce the margin you think it does? Check the labour rate. Take the labour dollars and divide by the hours.

Does the blended labour rate make sense? Check material recovery. Are you covering waste, handling, purchasing time, freight, and price movement? Check dollars per pound or dollars per ton. For structural steel or miscellaneous metals, does the final price make sense compared to similar past jobs? Check install hours. Did you include layout, unloading, access, anchors, welding, touch-up, cleanup, weather, and delays? Check exclusions. Are the unknowns protected? Check cash flow. Are there large material purchases up front? Do you need a deposit or progress billing? Check capacity. Do you actually have the people, equipment, and schedule space to do the job? And finally, check it with experienced people. Ask the shop foreman: “Does this look like enough hours?” Ask the installer: “What are we missing on site?” Ask the project manager: “What usually goes wrong on this type of job?” Ask purchasing: “Is this material price realistic and available?” Ask the owner: “How badly do we want this job?” That is how you build a stronger final number. The estimator does not need to know everything. But the estimator does need to know what must be known before the quote is sent.

That means asking better questions. Before final pricing, ask: Do we have complete drawings? Are dimensions missing? Is the material standard or special order? Are there long-lead items? Is the finish included? Is primer included? Is galvanizing included? Is paint touch-up included? Is engineering included? Are sealed drawings required? Is install included? Is access clear? Are lifts included? Who supplies anchors? Who supplies embeds? Who lays out the work? Who unloads material on site? Are there site orientations? Are there special safety requirements? Are work hours restricted? Is this regular time, overtime, night shift, or weekend work? Is travel included? Is there parking? Is there laydown area?

Are other trades in the way? Is the schedule realistic? Are we pricing from IFC drawings, tender drawings, or rough concept drawings? Are taxes included? Are bonds, insurance, or special paperwork required? Are we responsible for field measurements? Are we responsible for design? What is excluded? What is assumed? Those questions protect the final price because every unclear item can become a cost later. Now let’s talk about discounting. This is where margin gets destroyed fast. A customer says: “You are too high.” Or: “Can you sharpen your pencil?” Or: “We need you to take 10% off.” A bad estimator just cuts the price. A good estimator asks: “What are we removing?” Because when you reduce price without reducing scope, you are reducing margin. Let’s say the selling price is $100,000 with a 25% margin. That means the cost is $75,000 and the profit is $25,000. Now the customer asks for 10% off. If you reduce the price to $90,000 but the cost stays $75,000, your profit drops to $15,000. Your margin is now 16.7%.

You did not just give up 10%. You gave up 40% of the profit dollars. That is the part people miss. Discounting comes out of profit first. So instead of cutting blindly, negotiate by scope. You can say: “We can get closer to that number if galvanizing is removed.” “We can hit that target if install is by others.” “We can reduce the price if standard finish is accepted.” “We can provide that as an alternate.” “We can revise the price if the schedule is extended.” “We can reduce mobilization cost if the site is ready in one trip.” That is professional pricing. You are not being difficult. You are connecting price to scope. That is what good estimating does. Now here is the bigger lesson: The final price is not just a math result. It is a business decision. The spreadsheet can calculate the number. But the business has to decide if the job makes sense. Some jobs deserve high margin. Some jobs are good fill-in work. Some jobs are strategic. Some jobs build relationships. Some jobs should be avoided. Some jobs are traps. A high-risk job should not be priced the same as a clean, repeatable job.

A difficult customer should not get the same risk profile as a great customer. A shutdown job with liquidated damages should not be priced like a simple shop-only railing. A field-heavy job in bad weather should not be priced like bench fabrication. A job with missing drawings should not be priced like a fully detailed package. Your final price should reflect reality. Not optimism. Optimism is not a pricing strategy. Hope is not a contingency plan. So here is the process. First, build the true cost. Include material, labour, equipment, freight, subs, consumables, mobilization, supervision, project management, and job-specific costs. Second, use fully burdened labour rates. Do not use base wages. Do not use wishful hourly rates. Use rates that include burden, benefits, overhead, and realistic billable hours. Third, price the risk. Use contingency, allowances, clarifications, exclusions, or alternates. Do not leave uncertainty unpriced and unqualified. Fourth, apply margin correctly. Do not confuse markup with margin. To hit a target margin, divide cost by 1 minus the margin. Fifth, check the final number. Compare it to past work, labour hours, dollars per pound, market rates, and your internal break-even. Sixth, decide strategically. Ask whether this job is worth winning at this price. Seventh, protect the quote. Make sure the proposal clearly states inclusions, exclusions, assumptions, schedule, taxes, and any conditions.

That is how you build a final price. A lot of estimators think final pricing is just the last step. It is not. Final pricing is where the estimate gets tested. If the labour rate is wrong, the final price exposes it. If overhead is missing, the final price exposes it. If margin math is wrong, the final price exposes it. If the scope is unclear, the final price exposes it. If risk is ignored, the final price exposes it. And if you send the quote anyway, the job will expose it later. The goal is not to be the cheapest. The goal is not to be the highest. The goal is to be right. Right for the scope. Right for the risk. Right for the market. Right for the company. And right for the people who have to build the job. Because the shop has to live with the number you send. The field crew has to live with the number you send. The project manager has to live with the number you send. The owner has to live with the number you send. So do not treat final pricing casually. This is where you protect the business. This is where you stop guessing. This is where you stop saying, “We will make it up later.” Most of the time, you will not make it up later.

You make the money when you price the job correctly. You lose the money when you price the job incorrectly. The work just reveals which one happened. So when you build the final price, slow down. Check the cost. Check the labour rate. Check the overhead. Check the margin. Check the risk. Check the market. Check the proposal language. Then send the number with confidence. Because a good estimate is not just a number that wins the job. A good estimate is a number the company can actually build, deliver, invoice, and profit from. That is the difference between estimating work and protecting profit. And that is how you build the final price.
